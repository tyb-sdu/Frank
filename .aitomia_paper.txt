ABSTRACT: We present Aitomia, an agentic framework for AI-
driven atomistic and quantum chemical (QC) simulations that
helps experts and nonexperts alike set up and run calculations,
analyze results, and summarize them in textual and graphical forms
through natural language interaction. Built on the MLatom
software ecosystem, Aitomia supports AI-driven atomistic simu-
lations as well as conventional quantum-chemical calculations,
including density functional theory, semiempirical methods such as
GFN2-xTB, and selected high-level wave function-based methods,
through interfaces to widely used programs such as Gaussian,
ORCA, PySCF, and xtb, covering tasks from ground- and excited-state calculations to geometry optimization, thermochemistry, and
spectra simulations. By autonomously executing computational workflows, Aitomia can deliver infrared spectra in seconds and
reaction thermochemistry in minutes, with results close to experiment or high-level theoretical references while greatly reducing the
manual effort required from users. Aitomia lowers the barrier to performing atomistic simulations, thereby democratizing simulations
and accelerating research and development in the relevant fields.

INTRODUCTION
Computational chemistry has played a transformative role in
advancing chemical research over the past decades, enabling
spectra simulations and interpretation, the elucidation of
reaction mechanisms, and the generation of training data sets
for artificial intelligence (AI)-assisted design and discovery.
1−5
The backbone of such atomistic simulations is quantum
chemistry (QC) based on first-principles, solving the
Schro ̈ dinger equation by employing numerous approximate
methods that balance speed and accuracy. AI/machine learning
(ML) methods are also increasingly used for AI-driven
atomistic simulations. However, the growing sophistication of
computational chemistry tools presents significant challenges
for researchers.
Proficiency with these tools requires not only familiarity with
complex underlying theories and package-specific options but
also skills in Linux and access to high-performance computing
(HPC) resources. Moreover, simulating realistic chemical
processes often necessitates the integration of multiple
computational packages, compounding the complexity for
users across the chemical sciences. Despite progress in
automating computational workflows,
6−9
two critical barriers
remain. First, users must manually configure crucial simulation
parameters, often requiring consultation of extensive and
technical user manuals. Second, many workflows depend on
HPC resources, which are not readily available to non-
computational researchers, including experimental chemists
and students.
Some of the aforementioned issues are alleviated with
graphical user interfaces (GUIs) designed for the specific
software packages. For example, a popular Gaussian program
10
for QC calculations includes GaussView as its official GUI,
which facilitates the creation of input files and the analysis of
calculation results. Many other packages, such as Materials
Studio and ADF,
11
were built to provide seamless experiences
for computing job preparation, performing, and analyzing the
computations. Unfortunately, GUIs do not fully solve the
above problems, as researchers may still need to learn the
GUI’s options in addition to those of the underlying software,
submit jobs to HPC clusters, and seek expert advice.
Another useful solution lies in the growing number of web-
based platforms that provide simplified ways to submit and run
calculations, often with web GUIs; examples include
TeraChem
12
and ChemCompute.
13
We are also involved in
the development of similar platforms, Aitomistic Hub
14

While the above research shows major advances in LLM-

both AI/ML and QC simulations supporting a broad range of
typical tasks, from ground- and excited-state single-point
calculations to geometry optimizations and spectral simula-

framework that reduces technical and computational barriers,
enabling a wider audience to effectively leverage the computa-
tional chemistry tools.

automatically performing simulations and analyzing their
results in a clear, reusable format. Aitomia is leveraging the
MLatom ecosystem
9

atomistic and QC methods, as well as interfaces to various
powerful electronic-structure and ML programs such as
PySCF, Gaussian, ORCA, xtb, TorchANI, etc., enabling
essential computations such as energy calculations, geometry
optimizations, thermochemistry, reaction, and spectra simu-
lations. This framework enables experts and nonexperts alike,

RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
The Scope of Atomistic Simulations
Our goal is to design an intelligent assistant capable of
performing most key computational chemistry tasks using a
wide variety of state-of-the-art AI atomistic models and
conventional QC methods. This is an ambitious goal that
presents a formidable challenge, as it requires supporting many
different software packages and theoretical methods, both QC-
and AI-driven. Luckily, we can leverage the existing MLatom
software ecosystem,
9
which is particularly well-suited to this
goal because it seamlessly supports both QC and AI-driven
atomistic simulations. Hence, our intelligent assistant, Aitomia,
achieves its goal by leveraging MLatom’s comprehensive
feature set. The current capabilities of Aitomia are summarized
in Figure 2.
Currently, Aitomia can perform tasks such as single-point
calculations, geometry optimizations, frequency and thermo-
chemistry calculations, and simulations of infrared (IR) and
UV/vis spectra. For this, it leverages many state-of-the-art
pretrained universal ML models, such as the AIQM,
32−35
ANI,
36−39
OMNI-P,
40,41
and AIMNet
42
series. Some of these
models, such as the AIQM series and ANI-1ccx, approach the
coupled-cluster accuracy at a fraction of the computational cost
of DFT for many tasks. OMNI-P2x
41
is a unique universal
potential for excited-state calculations, furnishing UV/vis
absorption spectra with accuracy approaching that of TD-
DFT. Aitomia can also combine the single tasks into more
complex workflows, such as the calculation of the reaction
enthalpies, as we will discuss separately. The framework can be
further extended to other tasks supported by MLatom, e.g.,
MD, training, evaluating, and using custom ML models,
building data sets with active learning, and fine-tuning the
universal ML models for user-specific tasks.
Aitomia as an Assistant for Performing Standard
Computational Tasks
Typically, performing single computational tasks, such as
geometry optimizations or frequency calculations, requires
users to be familiar with the corresponding software input
formats and options. Learning them is a labor-intensive
process, impeding the widespread adoption of various software
and new techniques. To overcome this barrier, we have built
AI agents for Aitomia, which invoke LLMs to interact with the
user, choose an appropriate computational task requested by
the user, submit the computing job to the HPC server, retrieve
the output, analyze it, and report the results back to the user in
text and graphical format. Additionally, we implemented
quality-of-life features that enable Aitomia to retrieve
molecular structures from user-provided chemical names.
With Aitomia’s assistance, AI-driven atomistic and QC
calculations can be executed and analyzed autonomously,
with the input and output files and scripts organized in a clear
directory structure for the user’s review and results retrieval.
We show an example in Figure 3. The user interacts with
Aitomia and requests the structure of the hexanol molecule,
which Aitomia returns, allowing the user to analyze it (Figure
3A). Later, the user requests IR spectra calculations for this
molecule, and Aitomia calculates them using the AIQM2
method
35

the resulting spectrum is very close to the experimental
spectrum retrieved from the NIST Web site
43

Importantly, calculations of similar quality would take much
longer with DFT, whereas analogous spectra simulations with
semiempirical QM methods would not be as accurate.

English, Chinese, and Ukrainian, which we tested.

(e.g., the choice of AIQM2 in the above IR calculation)

Design of Autonomous Workflow and Execution with
Multiagent Aitomia
Beyond simplifying typical single computational tasks, the goal
of Aitomia is to assist in performing full computational
workflows comprising multiple tasks. We enable these
workflows via an autonomous, multiagent extension of
Aitomia. It is capable of designing workflows and executing
them with a minimal manual intervention.
First, the multiagent system evaluates the user’s intent to
determine whether a computational task is required. If
computation is needed, the system validates the relevant
computational parameters and, under the LangGraph-based
orchestration layer’s control, activates the executor agent. The
executor agent initializes the working directory, stores the user-
provided initial molecular structure (if available), constructs
the overall computational workflow, and dispatches subtasks to
subordinate agents.
Each subagent acquires the necessary contextual informa-
tion, including the working directory. If the user does not
provide an initial molecular structure, the subagent invokes a
structure-retrieval agent to obtain the required molecular
geometry. Within the structure retrieval agent, the molecular
species required for the current task are first identified. The
agent then determines whether the corresponding structure
should be retrieved from external sources or reused from
previously generated results. For external retrieval, the
molecule name is first used to query PubChem
47
via the
PUG REST API for a 3D conformer. If PubChem does not
return a structure, the name is resolved to a SMILES string via
the NIH Cactus Chemical Identifier Resolver,
48
and an initial
3D geometry is generated using RDKit
49
with ETKDG
conformer embedding and MMFF94 optimization. The
retrieved structure is then validated to ensure its chemical
plausibility by comparing its atom count to the expected count
from the molecular formula. If a mismatch is detected, then the
user is prompted to inspect the structure before the calculation
proceeds. The validated structure is then returned to the
calling subagent.
For subagents responsible for simpler tasks, they directly call
MLatom-based scripts to perform the required calculations. In
contrast, subagents handling more complex tasks further refine
the computational workflow and may invoke additional
specialized agents to decompose the task and execute specific
components.
After completing all assigned computations, each subagent
performs local result analysis and returns the processed outputs
to the executor agent. Once all subagent responses are
collected, the executor agent conducts an integrated analysis,
summarizes the key outcomes of the computation, records the
essential information in the system state, and terminates the
current computational workflow. The entire process concludes
with a report to the user summarizing either the results or the
encountered errors.
We will use the reaction energy for the Diels−Alder reaction
of cyclopentadiene and maleimide as a demonstration of the
application of the multiagent Aitomia. We only present the
main points of the chat between the user and Aitomia in Figure
5; complete model answers and other examples are provided in
the associated data (see the Data Availability section). In this
example, Aitomia completes the task in under 7 min and,
overall, calls single-task agents 12 times. The final reaction
energy of −33.7 kcal/mol is rather close to the best theoretical
estimate of ΔE = −34.2 kcal/mol in the GMTKN55
database,
50
due to the use of an accurate AIQM2 method,
which is orders of magnitude faster than DFT. Aitomia also
provides other thermochemical properties for this reaction, i.e.,
enthalpy and Gibbs free energy changes. For this reaction, the
stoichiometric coefficients are automatically determined from
the atom-conservation matrix of the optimized reactant and
product structures via null-space analysis, ensuring mass-
balanced reaction thermodynamics.
We provide additional examples in the associated data
(Examples F−T, Pages S7−S86, see the Data Availability
section), which, taken together, demonstrate Aitomia’s ability
to handle complex workflows.
We note that the multiagent system, e.g., the use of
multiagent Aitomia, requires extensive LLM-based reasoning
and, hence, is slower and more resource-intensive than using
Aitomia for single-task computations. Also, the autonomous
use of Aitomia for increasingly complex workflows is more
prone to occasional hallucinations and mistakes. To mitigate
the risk of arithmetic errors, Aitomia’s calculation agents
deterministically precompute energy differences in both
hartree and kcal/mol before passing the results to the LLM.
For example, reaction energies are reported in both units
directly by the calculation agent, so the LLM does not perform
the conversion itself. Absolute single-point energies are
reported in hartree only, as their values in kcal/mol are
physically meaningless. In general, the user is advised to
critically evaluate the responses from the multiagent Aitomia.

Benchmark on Atomistic Simulation Tasks
A major challenge for Aitomia is correctly interpreting user
queries, determining which information is missing and which
calculations are required, and finally organizing the corre-
sponding computational procedures to obtain reliable results.
Thus, we conducted a systematic benchmark of Aitomia on a
set of representative theoretical chemistry tasks, assigning each
task a difficulty level based on the degree of autonomy required
to answer the given questions. The full overview of the tasks is
provided in the associated data (the benchmark spreadsheet;
see Data Availability). Tasks with low autonomy typically
provide clear instructions regarding the type of calculations
needed and involve relatively simple execution with minimal
orchestration. Medium-level autonomy tasks require a higher
level of routing to organize multiple computational steps, but
still contain explicit instructions describing the necessary
procedures. In terms of high-level autonomy, little information
is provided to decide which tasks to perform, and the queries
are much closer to what we as computational chemists face
every day. It requires a deep understanding of the user’s
questions and the correct calculation procedure to obtain the
results. We used low and part of medium-level autonomy to
develop Aitomia; hence, they are marked “seen” in the
associated data’s benchmark spreadsheet, while all the others
were not seen by developers and are marked accordingly.

normally and the deviation between the results generated by

We note that, because the underlying quantum-chemical
calculations are deterministic, a successful optimization yields
the same geometry regardless of the trial run; the RMSD of
optimized structures across repeated trials is therefore
effectively zero. Any trial-to-trial variability in outcomes arises
solely from nondeterministic LLM decision-making, such as
selecting different quantum-chemistry engines (e.g., Gaussian
or PySCF), methods, or parameters across runs, or from the
structure-retrieval source changing when an external service is
intermittently unavailable and an alternative service returns a
different initial geometry.
It should be noted that, although challenging tasks
demonstrate increased flexibility, they are fundamentally
composed of basic agents. Consequently, complex tasks that
rely on fundamental computational capabilities not supported
by the benchmarked version of Aitomia�such as pK
a
calculations or solvation free energy evaluations that require
unimplemented capabilities of the base agents�were beyond
its scope.
In general, Aitomia achieves excellent stability on low-
difficulty tasks with a success rate approaching 100%. This
provides a solid foundation for more complex tasks composed
of these basic operations. For medium difficulty tasks, which
involve more complex workflows but still provide explicit
instructions, Aitomia achieves a success rate of 70.9%. In
contrast, the performance on high-difficulty tasks dropped to
45.6%. An examination of failed or partially successful cases
indicates that several systematic factors contribute to the
performance degradation.
One important factor is the absence of an automated self-
correction mechanism in the current implementation. The
present framework follows a relatively straightforward
execution pipeline in which intermediate computational results
are not actively validated. Consequently, errors introduced in
earlier stages can propagate to the final output. For example, if
a geometry optimization yields a structure with imaginary
frequencies, the workflow proceeds without triggering
reoptimization or structural correction, thereby compromising
the reliability of subsequent thermodynamic or vibrational
analyses. This behavior can be observed in several benchmark
cases (e.g., MED.EWG and HIGH.Gas.Vibrational; see the
benchmarks in the associated data spreadsheet for definitions
and prompts). Notably, inspection of the generated reasoning
indicates that the system often identifies the underlying source
of the error in its final analysis. This observation indicates that
integrating a self-correction module capable of diagnosing
common computational issues and invoking specialized
correction agents (e.g., imaginary-frequency removal or
workflow redesign) could substantially improve the system’s
robustness.
A second source of error arises from the molecular retrieval
stage, where the system occasionally fails to distinguish
between isomeric and identical structures correctly. When
the user-provided molecular description is ambiguous, the
system may incorrectly interpret identical species as different
isomers or, conversely, confuse distinct isomers as the same
structure. This issue is particularly evident in cases such as
HIGH.StericVsStereoelectronic. Even when the molecular
identity is described relatively clearly, occasional misassign-
ments still occur (e.g., MED.AxPref.StereoElec), suggesting
that the current molecular identification procedure lacks
sufficiently rigorous structural validation. In addition, this
problem affects cases involving different conformational states
of the same molecule (e.g., HIGH.CONF.HB-vs-nHB). When
the user does not explicitly provide a molecular structure, the
system retrieves molecules from a database based primarily on
the molecular name. However, such name-based retrieval
typically returns only a single reference geometry and does not
account for alternative conformations. Consequently, when
tasks require comparing structurally distinct conformers�such
as intramolecularly hydrogen-bonded versus non-hydrogen-
bonded conformers�the system may inadvertently use the
same retrieved structure for both calculations. This results in
identical optimized geometries and energies, precluding a
meaningful comparison. To address this limitation, we plan to
implement a more rigorous validation scheme comprising two
components: a graph-isomorphism check, in which the
molecular graph derived from the retrieved 3D structure (via
RDKit) is compared against the expected molecular graph to
verify bond connectivity and atom types; and a stereochemistry
verification, in which the 3D geometry is checked for
consistency with the expected chirality and double-bond
stereochemistry (e.g., using Cahn−Ingold−Prelog (CIP)
rules).
In several benchmark tasks, the QC calculations themselves
complete successfully, yet inaccuracies appear in the AI-
generated chemical interpretation. These issues mainly arise in
the post-analysis stage and typically involve minor errors in
spectral assignments or mechanistic interpretation. For
instance, in one of the benchmark runs (RUN7) of the
HIGH.AminoSubstitution test case, small inaccuracies appear
in the assignment of spectral features, while in the RUN10 of
the same test, the electronic transition assignments for benzene
were reversed. Although these issues do not affect the
underlying numerical calculations, they reduce the reliability
of the generated chemical explanations.
Another issue arises when the reaction equation is not
explicitly specified by the user. In some cases (e.g.,
MED.ΔGdeprot), the LLM may infer an incomplete reaction
scheme, such as omitting the H
+
species in deprotonation
calculations.
Finally, a small number of failures are attributable to
infrastructure-related issues such as instability of the LLM
service or unexpected server interruptions during task
execution. While these factors are external to the computa-
tional framework itself, their impact could be mitigated
through simple fault-tolerant mechanisms, such as automatic
task retry.

Taken together, these results suggest that Aitomia performs
robustly on well-defined computational chemistry tasks, while
its performance decreases in scenarios involving ambiguous
problem definitions, complex workflows, or automatic reaction
inference. The benchmark analysis highlights several promising
directions for future development including incorporating
automated self-correction mechanisms, improving molecular
validation procedures, and adopting more robust reasoning
strategies.
Aitomia Serves as a Look-Up Tool for Atomistic
Calculations
Even with a powerful, intelligent assistant at hand, to ensure
scientifically reliable results, users themselves must have
background knowledge of simulations, how they are
performed, and which QC methods or AI models are
appropriate for the task. Before the rise of LLMs, the only
ways were either learning from manuals and tutorials or from
human teachers. This severely limits the speed of knowledge
acquisition, which might also be limited to access. It is also
inconvenient to look up the information when needed in
lengthy manuals.
The challenge here is to create an LLM-driven lookup tool
that provides reliable information based on the domain
knowledge about the underlying MLatom capabilities exploited
by Aitomia. We solve this challenge by exploiting the Retrieval-
Augmented Generation (RAG) technique to improve the
LLM’s robustness by providing access to external knowledge
(MLatom documentation), thereby reducing hallucinations
and enhancing factual accuracy.
Aitomia, with access to the RAG database, provides a useful
chatbot expert in atomistic simulations that can assist
nonexperts in providing background information on available
computational approaches. Here, we show an example of

Limitations
Despite Aitomia’s demonstrated flexibility, the presented
capabilities are inherently constrained by the scope of the
integrated fundamental functional agents. At this stage,
Aitomia primarily focuses on establishing a robust foundation
for complex AI-driven atomistic and QC workflows, rather
than exhaustively covering all possible simulation tasks.
Aitomia operates by decomposing complex scientific
problems into sets of basic computational tasks. For example,
reaction enthalpy calculations are automatically formulated as
combinations of geometry optimization and frequency
calculations for multiple molecular systems with the overall
task structure orchestrated by LLMs. While this design offers a
high degree of generality and adaptability, the range of
problems that it can address is limited by the availability,
maturity, and reliability of these basic task modules.
Furthermore, the implementation supports only a limited set
of molecular input formats, primarily the XYZ and JSON
representations. This restriction limits interoperability with
existing simulation pipelines and user workflows, particularly
when alternative or more-specialized file formats are required.
Additionally, the integration of RAG primarily focuses on
answering MLatom-related queries, whereas the calculation
agents underutilize it.
A further group of limitations stem from how Aitomia
obtains and validates the molecular structures used in
calculations. The current version does not perform systematic
conformational sampling: calculations start from a single 3D
structure, either retrieved from PubChem or generated from a
SMILES, which may not correspond to the global minimum.
The same retrieval pipeline cannot yet produce chemically
valid geometries for coordination complexes or organometallic
compounds since standard databases and SMILES-based
generators do not handle these species, and the system lacks
specialized coordination-site assignment logic. Stereochemistry
is likewise handled only partially: explicit stereochemical
validation and enantiomer selection are not performed, so
that for an ambiguous query, the system silently adopts
whatever stereoisomer the database returns, and when the
PubChem lookup fails, and the RDKit fallback is triggered, the
NIH Cactus resolver may strip stereochemical information
from the SMILES before 3D-geometry generation, yielding an
arbitrary stereoisomer.
Outlook
The limitations and benchmark analysis presented above,
together with broader trends in agentic AI, point to several
directions in which Aitomia can be strengthened (Figure 7).
An important direction is the introduction of self-correcting
mechanisms. The benchmark analysis shows that a substantial
fraction of errors in the current implementation stem from the
lack of automated error detection and recovery. In practice,
many failures arise from common issues in the computational
chemistry workflows. For example, geometry optimizations
may converge to saddle points rather than true minima, as
indicated by the presence of imaginary frequencies in the
subsequent frequency calculations. In such cases, a self-
correction mechanism could automatically trigger additional
optimization steps, adjust computational methods or opti-
mizers, or regenerate improved initial guesses to obtain
physically meaningful structures. The modular, graph-based
architecture of Aitomia lends itself to integrating such
validation nodes without restructuring the overall workflow.
By enabling automatic validation of intermediate results and
adaptive recovery from both computational and system level
errors, self-correction mechanisms would substantially improve
the stability, robustness, and reliability of autonomous
computational workflows.
A further set of improvements targets the robustness of the
molecular input handling and validation. Planned additions
include automated conformer search and lowest-energy
conformer selection (e.g., via RDKit conformer enumeration
or CREST/GFN2-xTB); extended support for retrieving and
constructing chemically valid 3D structures of coordination
complexes and organometallic compounds, together with
dedicated coordination-chemistry reasoning; and CIP-based
stereochemical verification and explicit enantiomer selection,
in particular for ambiguous queries and for cases where the
structure retrieval fallback would otherwise discard chirality.
The scope of Aitomia can be broadened by expanding the
set of fundamental task agents to cover additional capabilities
supported by MLatom, such as MD simulations, custom ML
model training, active learning, and fine-tuning of universal ML
potentials. Broader support for molecular input formats would
further improve interoperability with existing simulation
pipelines. Because these capabilities are already available in
the underlying MLatom ecosystem, the primary effort lies in
designing reliable agent wrappers and appropriate default
parameters for each task type.
The relationship between Aitomia and MLatom is inherently
synergistic: the extensibility of MLatom enables continuous
expansion of Aitomia’s functionality, while the demands of
Aitomia provide practical motivation for advancing MLatom�
for instance, toward incorporating solvation effects and
developing machine-learning models for solvated systems.

CONCLUSIONS
We have introduced an intelligent AI assistant, Aitomia, that
helps researchers perform atomistic simulations tailored to
their needs even without specialized knowledge of the
underlying software. It draws on the versatility of the MLatom
software ecosystem to enable a broad range of computational
chemistry tasks, spanning both traditional QM (DFT, HF, and
post-HF; semiempirical methods) and AI atomistic models
(universal ML interatomic potentials and beyond).
Aitomia can assist with typical tasks such as geometry
optimizations of minima and transition states, frequency and
thermochemistry calculations, and simulations of IR and UV/
vis spectra. Because of the extensive use of AI atomistic
models, calculations are often much faster than DFT
calculations, with better or comparable accuracy. In addition,
Aitomia can autonomously design and execute complex
computational workflows, enabling convenient calculations of
reaction enthalpies, atomization energies, proton affinities, and
related quantities using single-task AI agents. Aitomia can also
be used as an expert advisor for performing atomistic
simulations. Its ability to analyze calculation results and answer
common QC and ML questions plays a promotional role,
helping professionals and nonprofessionals conduct computa-
tional research and analysis in related fields such as drug and
materials design.
By combining autonomous computational workflows with
natural language interaction and built-in result analysis,
Aitomia further contributes to the democratization of atomistic
simulations.

METHODOLOGY
LangGraph-Based Multiagent Architecture
In the implementation of Aitomia, single computational tasks
rely on the native tool calling capabilities of LLMs to parse
user-provided parameters and invoke the corresponding
computational functions. This version uses a lightweight,
robust logic with relatively low latency in language-level
parsing; consequently, the primary computational cost arises
from the calculations themselves. However, this design offers
limited flexibility and requires users to manually guide the
workflow step-by-step to complete the desired computations.
The Aitomia multientagent architecture is implemented
using LangGraph, and the overall structure of task execution is
illustrated in Figure 4. For the agent system, we adopted a
graph-controlled dynamic routing mechanism. Specifically, a
dedicated decision agent makes a judgment on which agent
should be called next by using an LLM tool to output a
structured decision. This decision is then returned to
LangGraph, allowing the graph engine itself to perform the
transition.
In this design, the LLM participates in dynamic decision-
making, but the execution flow remains explicitly governed by
the StateGraph, ensuring reproducibility and clear separation
between reasoning and control logic. The agents communicate
through graph nodes, with the system featuring a main
orchestrating agent responsible for analyzing user queries,
designing the workflow, and orchestrating execution by calling
upon corresponding single-task agents for specific calculations
and file management. Each single-task agent is dedicated to an
independent computational module (e.g., geometry optimiza-
tion or reaction simulations) and possesses its own internal
workflow. For simpler tasks (e.g., geometry optimization), the
workflow follows sequential logic. The agent prepares the
necessary inputs�such as acquiring molecular structures and
setting calculation parameters�in order, before finally
invoking the MLatom code to execute the calculation. For
more complex tasks (e.g., reaction energy calculation), the
agent employs a reasoning process and workflow design similar
to that of the orchestrating agent, involving the invocation of
various tools to manage complexity.
Interaction between Aitomia and the MLatom/HPC
Environment
Aitomia employs a hybrid architecture that combines tool
calling for MLatom integration with a node-local execution
model for HPC job management. In brief, the tool generates
MLatom input files or Python scripts from predefined
templates and saves them to the working directory, so the
user can later modify them. Then, it uses the command line to
run the input files or execute the Python script. In detail, the
workflow operates as follows:
Integration with MLatom. The connection between
Aitomia and the MLatom package is primarily achieved
through function calls. We adopted this approach because
LLMs are prone to hallucinations when generating code for
functionalities with an insufficient amount of training data.
Since computational stability is paramount, we chose not to
delegate code generation to an LLM; instead, we generated an
input file or a Python script from a predefined template for a
task.
The flexibility of our approach currently relies on a flexible
combination of single-task agents operating as described above.
This design offers a viable strategy for working with code
packages where the LLM may be undertrained or lack
sufficient exposure. While this approach inevitably reduces
some flexibility, we mitigate this by breaking tasks down into
smaller single-purpose units. One agent is responsible for one
specific granular task, and complex tasks are achieved by
orchestrating these smaller units.
MLatom provides the computational backbone for Aitomia
with dozens of interfaces and support for a wide range of
computational chemistry tasks. The integration with MLatom

Enhancing Model Robustness through RAG
Although the LLM can, at the date of writing, often provide
reasonable answers about computational chemistry and
machine learning, it currently often hallucinates due to the
vastness of the relevant knowledge. For example, it provides
wrong answers about the capabilities of MLatom, which is at
the core of our implementations. To address these issues and
improve the model’s performance, we also integrate RAG to
enhance Aitomia’s robustness as a lookup tool by providing
access to external knowledge, thereby reducing hallucinations
and improving factual accuracy (Figure 8). In Aitomia, external
knowledge is first chunked and then represented as
embeddings generated by the text-embedding-v3 model via
Aliyun’s API.
Traditional single-step RAG, while technically the simplest
and most feasible approach, is inaccurate for complex queries
because it performs only a single retrieval step before
generating an answer.
51,52
The multistep variant performs
much better on complex queries but is inefficient with simple
queries.
Adaptive-RAG presents a promising alternative,
53
which we
implemented in Aitomia. For straightforward queries, it can
answer them directly without consulting the database; for
simple queries, it checks the database only once to ensure
efficiency; and for complex queries, it can retrieve from the
database multiple times until the queries are fully answered.
53
When RAG is activated, Aitomia determines whether the
question is related to the database’s knowledge. If so, Aitomia
retrieves information from the RAG database. Then LLM
answers the user’s query based on the retrieved information.
After getting an intermediate answer, Aitomia judges whether
it is enough to answer the query. If not, Aitomia updates the
query and retrieves more information from the database.
Finally, Aitomia makes a summary and provides a complete
answer to the initial query.
By using this strategy, it retrieves only information relevant
to the database, thereby reducing the number of retrievals and
mitigating the model’s tendency to hallucinate. Therefore, we
integrate Adaptive-RAG to enhance the robustness of our
model. The integration of Adaptive-RAG reduces the
probability of model hallucinations and improves the model
performance based on user needs.

Deterministic Reliability Safeguards
Aitomia has several guards to prevent trivial errors such as
deterministic stoichiometry checks and unit conversions. For
example, for reaction thermochemistry, the LLM does not infer
stoichiometric coefficients; instead, the reaction agent
constructs an atom-conservation matrix from the optimized
reactant and product geometries and solves for the coefficients
mathematically via null space analysis (scipy.linalg.null_space).
If the reaction is atomically imbalanced or underdetermined,
the system returns an explicit error rather than proceeding with
incorrect coefficients. Unit conversions are handled in the
same deterministic manner: for relative energies, the
calculation agent precomputes the reaction energy in both
hartree and kcal/mol and reports both values, so that the LLM
receives the converted value directly and never performs the
unit arithmetic itself. Stereochemistry is likewise preserved
during structure retrieval whenever explicit descriptors are
supplied in the query: PubChem returns distinct, reproducible
conformers for the respective enantiomers so that, for example,
(R)- and (S)-limonene are retrieved and processed as separate
species.

Error Diagnosis
Aitomia includes an LLM-based error analysis module that is
triggered when a task fails. Aitomia aggregates the traceback
together with task-local.log, .err, and .out files from the current
working directory and uses this combined context to identify
likely causes of failure and suggest corrective actions. This
enables a more grounded diagnosis of bad input structures,
optimizer/convergence failures, and backend software prob-
lems.
